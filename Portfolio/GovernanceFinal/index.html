African Governance


By Sharita Underwood

African Governance

The traditions and institutions by which authority in a country is exercised. This includes the following three areas:

(A) the process by which governments are selected, monitored and

(B) the capacity of the government to effectively formulate and implement sound policies; and

(C) the respect of citizens and the state for the institutions that govern economic and social interactionsy among them

Each of the tree areas of governance was broken down into two measures which formed the six following dimensions of governance:

A


1) Voice and Accountability
2) Political Stability and Absence of Violence/Terrorism

B

3) Government Effectiveness
4) Regulatory Quality

C

5) Rule of Law
6) Control Corruption

Voice and Accountability

Capturing perceptions of the extent to which a country's citizens are able to participate in selecting their government, as well as freedom of expression, freedom of association, and a free media.

Political Stability and Absence of Violence/Terrorism

Capturing perceptions of the likelihood that the government will be destabilized or overthrown by unconstitutional or violent means, including politically‚Äêmotivated violence and terrorism.

Government Effectivenes

Capturing perceptions of the quality of public services, the quality of the civil service and the degree of its independence from political pressures, the quality of policy formulation and implementation, and the credibility of the government's commitment to such policies.

Regulatory Quality

Capturing perceptions of the ability of the government to formulate and implement sound policies and regulations that permit and promote private sector development.

Rule of Law

Capturing perceptions of the extent to which agents have confidence in and abide by the rules of society, and in particular the quality of contract enforcement, property rights, the police, and the courts, as well as the likelihood of crime and violence.

Control of Corruption

Capturing perceptions of the extent to which public power is exercised for private gain, including both petty and grand forms of corruption, as well as "capture" of the state by elites and private interests.

Several data sources were used to collect this data. Amongst those data sources were surveys of individuals or domestic firms, views of country analysts at various multilateral development agencies and data proveded by various nongovernmental organizations.

In all, over 200 countries were given a score between -2.5 and 2.5 in each of the six measurements of governance.
